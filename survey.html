Hi! 👋 Welcome to this survey about how algorithmic fairness is perceived. Algorithms and models for e.g. automated decision support is increasingly being used in society today. The survey is split into three parts: 1) First, we will ask a few questions about your background. 2) The second part examines when and how you think it is fair to use algorithms to make assessments and decisions that impact humans 👩‍👩‍👧‍👦. 3) The third part looks at some mathematical definitions of fairness, and here we examine how difficult the comprehension of different concepts of fairness are and which concept you would prefer. Section 5

Using algorithms
When we talk about fairness, it is important to know that sometimes there can be a skew in our data which could lead to unwanted behaviour in the model of e.g discrimination or favouritism. Imagine a case where – historically – a lot of male super figures have been investigated by the police👮‍♀️ and therefore all the villains 🦹‍♂️ have been found. On the other hand, female super figures have rarely been investigated by the police. Consequently, it looks as if there is a higher rate of villains among males than among females. Now, using these data, our model will think that a male is more likely to be a villain than a female is – but it might not be the truth. Another case could be that there is a genuine difference in crime rates between male and female super figures. However, this might be caused by different structures in society. This means that sometimes a model can inherit a skew from data or society brought about e.g. by prejudice, discriminations or mistakes in the collection of data.

Section 8

Fairness Criteria
In basic, there are many different quantitative measures for fairness, however, many measures cannot be achieved at the same time – which one is best, if any, depends on the context. So which trade-offs are the most important?🤷‍♀️ We are looking at a classification problem and we imagine we have a number of super figures and that we from an omniscient state know who are true heroes and who are true villains. When a model then makes predictions, we can - from our omniscient state - calculate how many of the true heroes were predicted as heroes, and how many were wrongly predicted, and the same goes for the true villains. We can also split these calculations into female and male groups. We can use these numbers to calculate different error rates. This was just a little background so let us get into it!👏
